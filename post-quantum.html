Application · PQC

Post-quantum transition is a governance programme — not a technology project.

The post-quantum transition is not only about selecting new algorithms. It is about knowing where cryptography is used, which systems and suppliers depend on it, who owns the change, what must happen first and how progress will be evidenced.

The Immediate Problem

The issue is readiness, not only algorithms.

Post-quantum cryptography has moved from theoretical concern to standards-based preparation. Finalised post-quantum standards, supervisory guidance, supply-chain pressure and financial-sector readiness signals mean organisations should now begin structured preparation.

The immediate problem is usually not algorithm replacement. The immediate problem is readiness.

Without those answers, late migration becomes compressed, expensive and operationally risky.

?What cryptography is in use?
?Which assets are exposed to quantum risk?
?Which systems support long-lived data?
?Which suppliers and vendors are involved?
?Which business services are dependent on affected cryptography?
?Which teams own remediation?
?What evidence will satisfy boards, auditors, regulators and customers?

Scope

Post-quantum transition affects more than security teams.

Post-quantum transition can touch every layer of the enterprise technology estate. This is why the transition must be governed as an enterprise programme, not managed as isolated technical projects.

Business Services

Customer-facing services and internal operations dependent on cryptographic trust

Supply Chain

Third-party software, hardware and vendor ecosystem dependencies

Regulatory

Supervisory guidance, reporting obligations and audit evidence requirements

Infrastructure

Certificates, protocols, libraries, cloud platforms and embedded systems

The questions will be about governance and evidence.

When boards, regulators and customers ask about PQC readiness, the questions will not be about algorithms. They will be about governance, process and defensible evidence.
