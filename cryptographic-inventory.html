The Challenge

Cryptography is spread across the entire enterprise technology estate.

Most organisations use cryptography across many layers of the enterprise technology estate. Without a cryptographic inventory, organisations cannot confidently plan PQC transition, manage certificate risk, assess algorithm exposure or evidence cryptographic resilience.

Governance Questions

What a cryptographic inventory should help answer.

The aim is not to create a static list. The aim is to create an operating view that supports governance.

02Where is it used?
03Which systems and business services depend on it?

CBOM

CBOM provides structure. Governance creates action.

A Cryptographic Bill of Materials can help represent cryptographic assets and their relationship to software components and systems. But CBOM is not the end state — the real value comes when data is connected to ownership, business context, risk decisions, remediation work and evidence.

CBOM Provides Visibility Into
  • Algorithms and cryptographic primitives
  • Certificates and trust chains
  • Cryptographic keys and key material
  • Network protocols (TLS, SSH, etc.)
  • Cryptographic libraries and versions
  • Software dependencies using cryptography
  • Deprecated or exposed cryptography
  • Areas requiring quantum-safe transition planning
CBOM → Navigator

CBOM and discovery data, when connected to ownership, business context, risk decisions, remediation work and evidence, becomes a living governance record.

Navigator Adds
  • Enriched cryptographic asset records
  • System and supplier context

  • Prioritisation and remediation tracking
  • Evidence capture and executive reporting
  • Continuous enrichment over time
